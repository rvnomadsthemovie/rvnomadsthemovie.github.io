Finding community and kinship on the road

Once you are away from the constraints of society, the experiences you will have and the people you will meet will amaze you.

You may be surprised that even when you are miles away from civilization, that you are never truly alone. The traveling community is always willing to lend a helping hand and support a fellow traveler in need.

Is it safe to travel on the road and camp alone?

While beginning travelers may stick to paid campsites and KOAs for safety, many seasoned RVers will boondock for the peace and quiet and to save money. Boondocking is camping off-grid without any hookup to city water, power, or sewer. While the East Coast and Midwest have limited opportunities to boondock, the West is wild and open. BLM (Bureau of Land Mangement) and National Forest lands are open to the public for camping and recreation. Most of these lands are found in Utah, Nevada, Arizona, Idaho, New Mexico, Montana, Wyoming, California, Oregon, and Washington. The only caveat is that you have to decamp and move your rig every 14 days or so.

What do you need to camp off-grid?

Before you tow your travel trailer out to the deserts of Nevada, you will have to prepare to survive or else you will prepare to die. You will need a source:

  • Water: Duh! The more the better. You need to figure out how much water your family uses per day to determine how many days you can stay off-grid. Your water use should include showering, washing the dishes, etc.
  • Food: Many of these camping spots are hours away from the nearest towns or grocery stores. You will need to stock up before you head out.
  • Electricity: You will need a source of electricity to run your refirgerator to keep your food fresh. As a boondocker, you will need to be your own power company. Electricity can be generated either with solar panels or a gas/diesel generator
  • Gas: You will need to have enough gas on hand to get in and get out to the nearest gas station as well as have enough gas to run your generator.
  • Propane: Most people will use propane for cooking, heating, and hot water. With a high powered and massive solar power system, it may be possible to cook and heat your RV with electricity. Such a system can be prohibitively expensive.
